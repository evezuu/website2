7.1 - introduction to air pollutants

objective :

  • identify the sources and effects of air pollutants

basics :

  • write about specific air pollutants not just generally

  • clean air act in 1970 give epa ability to monitor 6 air pollutants

  • required to set limits (basically can punish)

sulfur dioxide :

  • from coal combustion for electricity

  • respiratory irritant

  • smog

  • acid precipitation

nitrogen oxides :

  • nitric oxide and nitrogen dioxide

  • all ff combustion (gas)

  • warms ozone

  • contributes to photochemical smog

  • acid precipitation

carbon monoxide :

  • incomplete combustion

  • warms ozone

  • lethal to humans

lead :

  • metal plants

  • waste incineration

  • neurotoxicant

ozone (tropospheric) :

  • photochemical oxidation of nitrogen dioxide

  • respiratory irritant

  • smog

  • can cause plant damage

particulate matter :

  • ff + biomass combustion

  • respiratory irritant

  • smog

air pollutants vs. greenhouse gasses :

  • carbon dioxide is not one of the six criteria pollutants

  • in 2007 the supreme court ruled the epa could regulate greenhouse gases so it did in 2009 (bc it's harmful to humans)

  • carbon dioxide does not DIRECTLY lower air quality from a human health standpoint

  • not toxic to organisms bc it's not damaging lung, eyes, leading to smog, decreased visibility

  • leads to earth warming + environmental change DOES lead to human health consequences (basis for supreme court ruling in 2007)

  • in apes, carbon dioxide is not an air pollutant

coal combustion :

  • releases more air pollutants than any other ffs (35% of global electricity)

  • releases carbon monoxide, carbon monoxide, sulfur dioxide, nitric oxide, nitrogen dioxide, mercury, arsenic, lead, and pm (which carries toxic metals)

  • sulfur dioxide is released a lot in coal

  • it is a respiratory irritant which worsens asthma + bronchitis

  • creates sulfur aerosols which reflect the sun (lowers photosynthesis)

  • forms sulfurous (grey) smog

  • combines with water + oxygen in atmosphere to form acid rain

nitric oxides :

  • released by combustion of anything (specifically ffs + biomass)

  • nitric oxide forms when nitrogen combines with oxygen

  • can become nitrogen dioxide if it reacts with ozone or oxygen again

  • sunlight converts nitrogen dioxide back into nitric oxide

impacts :

  • respiratory irritant

  • leads to ozone formation, photochemical smog

  • combines with water + oxygen in atmosphere to form nitric acid, acid precipitation

epa + lead :

  • before caa (clean air act), lead was a common gasoline additive

  • epa began to phaseout lead from gasoline in 1947

  • vehicles made after 1974 had to have catalytic converters to reduce nitric oxide, nitrogen dioxide, carbon monoxide and hydrocarbon emissions

  • also a neurotoxicant to humans (damages nervous system)

primary air pollutants :

  • emitted directly from sources

  • e.g. vehicles, power plants, volcanoes, forest fires

  • nitric oxide, nitrogen dioxide, carbon monoxide, carbon dioxide, vocs (volatile organic carbons), sulfur dioxide, particulate matter, hydrocarbons

secondary air pollutants :

  • have transformed in presence of sunlight, water, or oxygen

  • occur more during the day bc of the sun

  • tropospheric ozone

  • sulfuric acid + sulfate

  • nitric acid + nitrate

7.2 - photochemical smog

objective :

  • explain the causes and effect of photochemical smog and methods to reduce it

precursors :

  • ingredients for the smog

  • nitrogen dioxide which is broken by sunlight into nitrogen oxide + oxygen

  • oxide + oxygen = ozone

  • volatile organic compounds (hydrocarbons) that bind with nitric oxide to form photochemical oxidants

  • carbon-based compounds evaporating easily = gustation (e.g. gasoline, cleaning fluid, oil-based paints)

  • ozone forms when nitric oxide is broken down by sunlight into nitrogen oxide + oxygen = ozone

  • respiratory irritant in troposphere at earth's surface

  • damaging to plant stomata, limiting growth

conditions :

  • sunlight drives ozone formation bc more nitrogen dioxide is broken down

  • also vocs evaporate faster so more smog formation

normal ozone formation :

  • early morning traffic produced nitrogen dioxide

  • sunlight breaks it into nitric oxide (no) + oxygen atom (o)

  • the oxygen atom bonds with oxygen so ozone forms

  • normally ozone formation peaks in afternoon, and at night the ozone breaks down and reforms nitrogen dioxide (cancels out)

photochemical smog formation :

  • the normal ozone production stuff

  • vocs bond with nitric oxide to form photochemical oxidants

  • ozone cannot recombine with the nitric oxide bc vocs alr did

  • leads to ozone buildup

  • ozone + photochemical oxidants = photochemical smog

factors that increase formation of photochemical smog :

  • increased traffic bc more nitrogen dioxide

  • higher vocs emissions (e.g. gas stations, laundromats)

  • more sunlight bc more ozone

  • warmer temperatures bc vocs evaporate faster

  • urban areas have more smog bc they have all of these factors

  • more traffic, more nitrogen dioxide

  • hotter temperatures bc low albedo of blacktop

  • more vocs due to gas stations + factories

  • more electricity demand so more nitrogen dioxide and nitric oxide emissions

environmental impacts :

  • reduces sunlight so less photosynthesis

  • damages stomata + irritates animal respiratory tracts

human impacts :

  • for humans it worsens asthma, bronchitis, copd, eyes

economic impacts :

  • increased health care costs to treat these

  • lost productivity bc sick workers miss work or die

  • decreases agricultural yields bc less sunlight reaching crops + damage to plant stomata

vehicle reduction :

  • decreasing amt. of vehicles on the road (less nitrogen dioxide emissions)

  • fewer vehicles = less gas = less vocs

energy reduction :

  • increased electricity production from renewable sources bc no nitrogen dioxide + nitric oxide emissions

  • natural gas power plants release a lot less nitrogen dioxide + nitric oxide than coal

7.3 - thermal inversion

objective :

  • describe thermal inversion and its relationship with pollution

urban heat island effect :

  • urban areas are hotter than surrounding suburban + rural areas

  • lower albedo bc concrete + asphalt absorb more of the sun’s energy than areas with more vegetation

  • absorbed sunlight is given off as infrared radiation

  • vegetation reflects more sunlight

  • evapotranspiration = water evaporating from surfaces and transpiration from plants carries heat from surface into the atmosphere

  • cools off rural + suburban areas with more vegetation

thermal inversion :

  • normally the atmosphere is warmest at earth’s surface + cools as altitude rises

  • basically it just switches but cold air is still at the top

  • bc of a warm air mass blocks the cold air

  • it goes cold air -> warm air -> cooler air

  • also bc of hot urban surfaces cooling overnight

  • bc warm air rises air convection carries air pollutants away from earth’s surface

effects :

  • air pollutants are trapped closer to earth

  • respiratory irritation = asthma flare ups leading to hospitalizations, worsened copd, emphysema (obstructive lung disease)

  • decreases tourism revenue

  • decreased photosynthesis

7.4 - atmospheric carbon dioxide and particulates

natural sources :

  • lightning strikes convert n2 in atmosphere into nitrogen dioxide + nitric oxide

  • forest fires make carbon monoxide, particulate matter, nitrogen dioxide + nitric oxide

  • plants emit volatile organic compounds (e.g. terpenes + ethylene from pine, fir, spruce, etc. + smoky mountains)

  • volcanoes emit sulfur dioxide, particulate matter, carbon monoxide, and nitrogen oxides

living sources :

  • respiration bc everything releases carbon dioxide through respiration

  • not that major though

  • natural particulate matter sources = sea salt, pollen, ash

  • leads to haze (scattering of sunlight + reduced visibility)

  • aerobic decomposition = decomposition of organic matter by bacteria in the presence of oxygen it releases carbon dioxide

  • anaerobic decomposition = decomposition of organic matter by bacteria decomposers in low or oxygen-free conditions + releases methane

particulate matter :

  • any solid or liquid particles suspended in air (also called particulates)

pm10 :

  • pm10 micrometers

  • particles or droplets like dust, pollen, ash

  • too small to be filtered out by nose hairs + trachea cilia

  • can irritate respiratory tract + cause inflammation

pm2.5 :

  • pm2.5 micrometers

  • more concerning bc can go deeper into lungs

  • associated with chronic bronchitis + increased risk of lung cancer

  • generally degrade lungs + respiratory tract

7.5 - indoor air pollutants

objective :

  • identify indoor air pollutants

developing countries :

  • developing nations rely on subsistence fuels (wood, manure, charcoal)

  • biomass releases carbon monoxide, particulate matter, nitrogen oxides, and volatile organic compounds (can also cause deforestation)

  • often combusted indoors with poor ventilation

  • estimated that 3 billion people globally cook with subsistence fuels

  • leads to 4 million deaths annually

developed countries :

  • developed nations use more commercial fuels (coal, oil, natural gas)

  • burned in well ventilated areas (e.g. furnaces, stoves)

  • major indoor air pollutants in developing countries is from cleaning supplies, insulation, lead paint

pm + asbestos :

  • e.g. smoke from indoor biomass combustion + asbestos

  • asbestos is a long, silicate particle that was used in insulation

  • now it is linked in lung cancer

  • phased out of use but in older buildings

  • not dangerous until asbestos particles enter air

  • should be removed by professionals with ventilation in the area

  • plastic to seal off area from rest of building

carbon monoxide :

  • produced by incomplete combustion of any fuel source

  • asphyxiant = causes suffocation due to binding to hemoglobin in blood which displaces oxygen

  • lethal to humans in high concentrations especially with poor ventilation

  • developed nations = can be detected by carbon monoxide detectors

  • developing nations = emitted from biomass combustion for heating/cooking

volatile organic compounds :

  • chemicals used in variety of home products that easily vaporize, enter air, and irritate stuff

  • adhesives/sealants = chemicals used to glue carpet down, hold furniture together + have formaldehyde (carcinogenic)

  • cleaners = febreeze, ecolab

  • plastics also can

radon gas :

  • radioactive gas released by decay of uranium naturally in rocks underground

  • enters home through foundationary cracks + disperses up

  • can also seep into groundwater sources + enter body through drinking water

  • second leading cause of lung cancer after smoking

  • epa recommends testing homes with airborne radon monitor

  • sealing cracks in foundation can prevent it from entering + increasing ventilation in the home can disperse if detected

dust + mold :

  • dust = dead human skin cells

  • settles naturally in homes, can enter respiratory tract

  • mold develops in areas that are dark and damp and aren’t well ventilated

  • black mold releases spores in air which is harmful to the respiratory system

  • can be removed by physically cleaning it out + fixing the leak forming it

lead :

  • found in paint in old homes

  • paint chips off walls/windows and is eaten by small children

  • or inhaled as bust

  • lead water pipes can also release lead into drinking water sources

  • less common than lead paint

  • in children it is a neurotoxicant

7.6 - reduction of air pollutants

objective :

  • explain how air pollutants can be reduced at the source

reducing emissions :

  • drive less, walk/bike/bus mor

  • conserve electricity

  • eat more plants, less meat

  • renewable energy (solar, wind, hydroelectric)

clean air act :

  • allows epa to set levels for air pollutants

  • monitor emissions and hurt companies who don’t comply

cafe vehicle standards :

  • corporate average fuel economy

  • requires vehicle manufacturers to work to make more efficient vehicles

  • more efficiency vehicles burn less gasoline

pollution credits :

  • if you bring levels low you get credits

  • incentivises to release few credits

  • how tesla is profitable bc you can sell the credits

vapor recovery nozzle :

  • captures hydrocarbon volatile organic compounds released from gasoline

  • reduces volatile organic compounds (less smog + irritants)

  • also reduces benzene (carcinogenic compound)

catalytic converter :

  • required on all vehicles after 1975

  • filters exhaust coming out of engine

crushed limestone :

  • for sulfur

  • made of calcium carbonate

  • gets trapped as calcium sulfate

  • can be used for gypsum wallboards or sheetrock

fluidized bed combustion :

  • for nitrogen

  • blasting in jets of air

  • changes the nitrogen into a bubbling bed

  • allows coal to be combustion at lower temperatures (less nitrogen oxides)

dry scrubbers :

  • large column with chemicals that reacts with air pollutants

  • traps the air pollutants

wet scrubbers :

  • has some chemical agent that traps the chemicals

  • also has a mist apparatus

  • grabs on to particulate matter

electrostatic precipitator :

  • power plant emissions go through a device which gives the particles a negative charge + then they stick to positively charged plates to be trapped

baghouse filter :

  • large fabric bag filters that trap particulate matter as air from industrial processes

  • pm is collected then taken to the landfill

7.7 - acid deposition

objective :

  • describe acid deposition

  • describe the effects of acid deposition on the environment

sources of nitrogen oxides + sulfur dioxide :

  • primary pollutants that cause most acid precipitation

  • sulfur dioxide = coal fired power plants, diesel

  • nitrogen oxides = vehicle emissions

limiting acid rain :

  • higher cafe standards

  • more public transit

  • renewable energy source

  • more efficient electricity use

  • since clean air act acid deposition increased

chemistry :

  • no2 + so2 react with o2 and water in the atmosphere forming nitric + sulfuric acid

  • basically the acids can separate into ions which then join the rain

soil/water acidification :

  • acidity = higher h+ on concentration, lower ph

  • h+ displace nutrients from the soil

  • also make toxic metals more soluble in water

  • can slow growth or kill plants + animals living in the soil or water

mitigating acid rain :

  • limestone is a natural base that can neutralize acidic soil/water

  • calcium carbonate reacts with h+ ions to neutralize (move ph closer to seven) the acidic water

  • regions have natural limestone in bedrock (natural)

  • humans can simulate this by spreading limestone powder over things

  • acid rain can also corrode human structures

  • obviously limit so2 + nox

7.8 - noise pollution

objective :

  • describe human activities that result in noise pollution and its effects

urban noise pollution :

  • any sound loud enough to cause physiological stress

  • construction, transportation, manufacturing plants, domestic activity (loud neighbors, lawn mowing, home projects)

wildlife effects :

  • animals can also suffer from hearing loss

  • caterpillars hearts beat faster when exposed to highway noise pollution

  • lack of hearing can also make predators not find prey

aquatic effects :

  • from ship engines, sonar, oil, gas ships

  • physiological stress = faster heart rate, hearing loss

  • whales rely on vocal communication so hearing loss = deadly

  • seismic surveying = ships sending huge air blasts into the water to see if there is oil + is really loud

  • obviously disrupts the aquatic life bc causes hearing loss
