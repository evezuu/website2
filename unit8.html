Unit 8: Aquatic + Terrestrial pollution

8.1 - point and nonpoint pollution sources

objective :

  • identify differences between point and nonpoint sources of pollution

point sources :

  • pollutant that enters environment

  • an easily identified and confined place

  • you can “point” to it

nonpoint sources :

  • epa defines this as many pollutants at once

  • e.g. an estuary being polluted bc multiple rivers entering

point sources examples :

  • animal waste runoff from a cafos

  • emissions from power plants

  • bp oil spill (hydrocarbons + benzene)

nonpoint sources examples :

  • urban runoff collects multiple pollutants

  • pesticides again have multiple different types of chemicals

pollutants :

  • specific chemicals or groups of chemicals that come from SPECIFIC sources with specific environmental + human health effects

  • on frqs

  • memorize lots of pollutant names 

  • their sources, environmental + human effects, their mitigation strategies

pollution :

  • vague, nondescript term for any substance that’s bad for the environment

  • not on frqs

  • specific types are okay (e.g. thermal, noise, sediment) bc they have effects

8.2 - human impacts on ecosystems

objective :

  • describe the impacts of human activities on aquatic ecosystems

range of tolerance :

  • human actions can change it

  • e.g. ph, temperature, salinity, sunlight

  • also range of tolerance for different pollutants

  • pollutants cause limited growth, limited reproduction, difficulty respiring, hormonal disruption, death

  • explain specific effects

acid rain :

  • ph decreases means that range of tolerance also does

  • can cause disrupted blood osmolarity + aluminum toxicity

  • indicator species show if ecosystem is acidic or not

  • e.g. high crustacean = ph > 6 + high whitemoss = ph < 6

temperature tolerance of reef algae :

  • mutualistic relationship between coral + algae

  • algae have narrow temperature tolerance and leave the reef when temperature rises

  • not only bleaches coral but also algae dies

human impacts on coral reefs :

  • ghgs (warm ocean temperature + bleaching coral)

  • overfishing + bottom trawling can break coral reef + make water turbid

  • sediment pollution = all solid matter in the pollutants

  • e.g. sand, gravel + makes water increase turbidity

  • toxicants = made by humans

  • can be lethal to coral reef ecosystems

  • nutrients (e.g. ammonia, cafos) can lead to eutrophication and runoff

oil spills :

  • hydrocarbons in crude oil are toxic to marine organisms and can kill them

  • decreased visibility + decreased photosynthesis

  • oil sticking to bird feathers or sinking to kill bottom-dwellers (toxicity + asphyxiation)

  • can also decrease industry (e.g. tourism, fishing, seafood production)

  • oil can poison the plants in the estuary meaning that fish cannot reproduce

clean up :

  • can happen from underwater well explosion 

  • tanker carrying oil can explode

  • can use booms to prevent oil from spreading (quickest + immediate)

  • physical removal

  • synthetic chemicals can be harmful but do get rid of it

  • pollution -> more pollution

8.3 - endocrine disruptors

objective :

  • describe endocrine disruptors

  • describe the effects of endocrine disruptors on ecosystems

definition :

  • any compound that can affect hormonal system of animals

  • chemicals the mimic hormones (unintentional + intentional)

  • they disrupt the normal functioning of the endocrine system

  • if it is meant for humans obvi not for humans

  • can transfer through sewage

  • e.g. atrazine binds to receptors of cells that should convert estrogen into testosterone leading to gender balances

specifics :

  • obvi atrazine bc crop runoff and can groundwater

  • ddt is an broad-spectrum insecticide that was phased out bc really bad

  • still in environment

  • phthalates are the plastic compounds

  • can enter surface or groundwater through trash, chemical waste

  • lead, arsenic, mercury = heavy metals

  • human medications should not be used for animals

mercury :

  • naturally occurring in coal

  • released by coal combustion + trash incineration

  • attaches to pm and deposits in soil + water wherever it settles

  • endocrine disruptor bc interferes with menstrual cycle

  • teratogen can damage fetus brain

  • pregnant woman should limit exposure to mercury (no seafood)

arsenic :

  • naturally occurring element

  • can be exposed by mining and can infiltrate groundwater

  • used in rat poison

  • anthropogenic sources in pesticides (wood treatment)

  • carcinogenic + endocrine disruptor

  • can be removed with water filters

lead :

  • old paint in homes, water pipes, and pm form coal combustion

  • neurotoxicant (inorganic substance the damages cns)

  • endocrine disruptor but removed with water filters

coal ash :

  • can be a source of mercury, lead, and arsenic

  • fly ash = pm carrying the metal that go into ecosystems far away

  • bottom ash = bottom of furnace in ash ponds which has a ton off bad stuff temporary but can overflow and go into nearby sources (groundwater)

8.4 - human impacts on wetlands

objective :

  • describe the impacts of human activity on wetlands and mangroves

wetlands :

  • an area with soil submerged in water for at least part of the year but shallow enough for emergent plants

  • wetland plants adapted to living with submerged roots

  • ecosystem services = habitats for animal + plant foods, regulating groundwater, sequestration of carbon dioxide, filtering water

  • even tourism or research

threats to wetlands :

  • pollutants = nutrients (nitrogen, phosphorus), sediment, motor oil, pesticides, endocrine disruptors

  • development bc can be changed into other land

  • water diversion upstream for flood control

  • dam construction reduces the nutrients to the wetlands

  • overfishing messes up predatory-prey dynamic

benefits of mangroves :

  • provide 800 billion dollars

  • can be used for fuel

  • protects the coast (more effective than a wall)

  • water filtration

  • tourist attractions bc of lots of fish there

  • sequestration of carbon dioxide

threats :

  • logging, agriculture, aquaculture, pollution

  • coastal development for like hotels

  • climate change can make them extinct

solutions :

  • cover crops (crop to stop runoff into water)

  • better animal manure management

8.5 - eutrophication

objective :

  • explain the environmental effects of excessive use of fertilizers and detergents on aquatic ecosystems

process :

  • starts from extra nitrogen + phosphorus bc they fuel algae growth

  • algae bloom covers surface of water, blocking sunlight + kills plants below

  • algae die-off and bacteria break down algae (aerobic process)

  • lowers oxygen levels and kills more animals

  • bacteria use up more oxygen 

  • creates a positive feedback loop: less o2 -> more dead org. -> more bacterial decomposition -> less o2 -> hypoxia

cultural eutrophication :

  • anthropogenic nutrient pollution that leads to eutrophication 

  • cultural = human caused

  • major sources = discharge from sewage, animal waste from cafos + synthetic fertilizer from agricultural fields + lawns

oligotrophic waterways :

  • low nutrient levels, high dissolved oxygen

  • can be due to age of water body

  • aquatic ecosystems naturally undergo succession

  • sediment buildup on bottom (benthic zone) leads to higher nutrient level

  • oligotrophic -> mesotrophic -> eutrophic

dissolved oxygen + dead zones :

  • decrease in dissolved oxygen (hypoxia) = dad zone

  • all aquatic life requires dissolved oxygen for respiration

  • fewer do = fewer species

example :

  • during the spring sun-heated runoff cuts off contact with oxygen

  • nitrogen and phosphorus from soil due eutrophication

  • starved of oxygen and cut off means organisms at the bottom die

8.6 - thermal pollution

objective :

  • describe the effect of thermal pollution on aquatic ecosystems

solubility of oxygen + temperature :

  • solubility = ability of a matter to dissolve into a liquid

  • inverse relationship between water temp + oxygen solubility (water temp increase + do decrease)

thermal pollution :

  • when heat released into water

  • heat increases respiration rate (thermal shock)

  • hot water = less o2

  • suffocation

sources :

  • power plants use cool water from nearby water then return it back hotter

  • steel mills, paper mills, use cool water to warm machinery

  • urban runoff can be hot bc blacktop + low albedo so then thermal pollution

  • nuclear power plants require LOTS of cool water for reactor core (really bad)

cooling towers :

  • used to cool steam back into water + to hold warmed water before returning to local surface water

  • standard in nuclear power plants so in frqs say increase efficiency of them

8.7 - persistent organic pollutants (pops)
objective :

  • describe the effect of persistent organic pollutants on ecosystems

pops :

  • persistent (long-lasting) organic (carbon-based) pollutants

  • synthetic (human-made) compounds that do not easily breakdown in the environment and accumulate and build up in water + soil

  • fat-soluble meaning the also accumulate and persist in animals fat tissue

  • can slowly be released from fatty tissue into blood stream which can impact brain + other organs over time (reproductive system)

examples + sources :

  • ddt (outdated insecticide)

  • even after phaseout still found

  • pcbs (plastic/paint additive)

  • pbde (fire-proofing)

  • bpa (plastic additive)

  • dioxins (fertilizer production + combustion of waste + biomass

  • byproduct of fertilizer

  • 90% of human dioxin exposure comes from animal fats bc fat tissue buildup

  • phthalates (plastics)

  • perchlorates (rocket/missile fuel, firework)

transport of pops :

  • pcbs are released into aquatic ecosystems by industrial wastewater

  • toxic to fish (endocrine disruptor)

  • reproductive failure = cancer in humans

  • human exposure comes through animal products

perchlorates :

  • common near military testing sites

  • remain in soil and can leach into groundwater or runoff into surface water

travel :

  • pops travel long distances through wind + water

  • wastewater release from industrial processes, leachate from landfills

  • enter soil/water -> eaten by animals -> stored in their fat -> eaten by humans OR taken in by drinking water

8.8 - bioaccumulation and biomagnification

objective :

  • describe bioaccumulation + biomagnification

  • describe the effects of bioaccumulation and biomagnification

bioaccumulation :

  • absorption and concentration of compass (pops) in the cells + fat tissues of organisms

  • methylmercury doesn’t dissolve easily in water they  don't enter blood easily + can’ t leave the body

  • build up in fat tissue means that they reach higher + higher concentrations in the organisms over time

biomagnification :

  • happens bc of bioaccumulation

  • increasing concentration of fat-soluble compounds like methylmercury and pops in each level up the trophic pyramid or food web/chain

  • begins with pops

  • primary consumers take in pops y earring producers

  • then secondary consumers have them

  • bc of 10% rule organisms at the next trophic level need to eat more food so more pop level

  • e.g. salmon, dolphins, whales

biomagnification (ddt) :

  • ddt = banned insecticide but still in sediments of water

  • taken in by bottom feeders (primary consumer) and goes up each trophic level

  • even if extremely low levels they have to eat more so there is more for large predators

  • the levels can get high enough to have thinning of eggshells in the birds

methylmercury :

  • emitted through coal + volcanoes + is carried by wind then deposited in water where bacteria converts it into toxic methylmercury

  • taken in by phytoplankton

  • richest higher levels but is a neurotoxicant 

  • e.g. tuna, sharks, whales

  • human exposure to methylmercury + pops comes from eating large predatory fish

  • damages human nervous system + developing fetus

  • disrupts reproductive 

8.9 - solid waste disposal

objective :

  • describe solid waste disposal methods

  • describe the effect of solid waste disposal methods

types + sources :

  • msw (municipal solid waste)

  • solid waste from cities 

  • e.g. trash, litter, garbage, refuse (apes word for trash)

  • waste “stream” = flow of solid waste to recycling centers, landfills, or trash incineration facilities

  • e-waste = old computers, tvs phones, tablets

  • only 2% of msw but have hazardous waste

  • can leach endocrine disruptors

  • should be disposed of in special facilities

sanitary landfills :

  • apes word for developed countries “landfills”

  • clay/plastic bottom liner which prevents pollutants from leaking into soil + groundwater

  • leachate collection system = system of tubes at bottom to collect leachate (water draining through waste + carrying pollutants) for treatment + disposal

  • methane recovery system = tubes to collect methane + can be used for heat

  • clay cap = mixture used to cover landfill to keep out animals, keep in smell + allow vegetation to regrow

landfills contents + decomposition :

  • low levels of decomposition

  • bc low o2, moisture, + organic matter

  • since these factors are rarely present together in landfills little decomposition occurs + remain the same size as they started

NO landfill :

  • hazardous waste (antifreeze, motor oil, cleaners, electronics, car batteries)

  • metals (copper + aluminum)

  • old tires can collect water and be breeding ground for mosquitos

YES landfill :

  • cardboard/food wrappers + can’t be recycled

  • rubber, plastic films

  • styrofoam

  • food, waste + paper can go into landfills but should be recycled/composted

landfill issues :

  • have environmental impacts like groundwater contamination + release of ghgs

  • groundwater can be contaminated if leachate leaks

  • ghgs come from decomposition -> global warming + climate change

  • nimby (not in my back yard) nobody wants a landfill in their backyard

  • bc of smell + sight, can attract animals, and contamination of groundwater

  • landfills are often placed near low-income or minority communities bc they don’t have resources to advocate against them

waste incineration :

  • reduce volume that needs to be landfilled

  • can reduce up to 90% but releases air pollutants

  • bottom ash may have toxic metals + is stored in ash ponds

  • toxic metals can leach out of storage ponds or be released into the atmosphere

  • can be burned for electricity bc heat

ocean dumping :

  • occurs in some countries 

  • usually no environmental regulation

  • plastic especially collects into large floating garbage patches

  • e.g. great pacific garbage patch

  • can suffocate animals

8.10 - waste reduction methods

objective :

  • describe changes to current practices that could reduce the amount of generated waste and their associated benefits and drawbacks

reduce, reuse and recycle :

  • reducing consumption is the most sustainable bc decreases natural resources harvested + the energy inputs to creating, packaging, and shipping good

  • e.g. metal/reusable water bottle, riding bike or walking

  • reusing = next best bc it doesn’t add more energy

  • e.g. buying second hand clothes, reusing furniture

  • recycling = processing + converting solid waste material into new products

  • e.g. anna’s recycled shoes

  • third r bc least sustainable but better than throwing away

benefits :

  • reduces demand for new materials, especially metals + woods

  • reduces energy required to ship raw materials + produce new product (less ff)

  • reduces landfill volume, obvi conserving space + reducing need for more landfills

drawbacks :

  • costly + requires energy

  • cities that offer recycling service need to process sort + sell collected materials

  • prices change rapidly leading to recycled materials being thrown away often

  • when citizens recycle items that shouldn't be it increases the cost for cities to sort + process

composting :

  • can be done with organic matter

  • decomposition under controlled conditions

  • reduces landfill volume + produces rich organic matter

  • produces valuable product to sell (compost)

  • reduces amt. of methane release by anaerobic decomposition or organic matter

  • should be done w/ proper mix of carbon to nitrogen (30:1)

  • should also be aerated + mixed to optimize decomposition (bacteria need o2 for decomposition)

  • drawbacks = smell, and potential unwanted animals

e-waste :

  • waste from electronics (e.g. phones, computers, etc.)

  • have heavy metals (e.g. lead, mercury, cadmium)

  • can leach these toxic metals

  • developed countries sell e-waste to developing countries where workers are exploited which leads to toxic metals in their ecosystems

waste -> energy :

  • waste = easily combustible

  • can generate electricity

  • same process as burning coal, natural gas, biomass

  • methane gas produced by decomposition can be collected w/ pipes + burned for electricity

  • same process

  • reduces landfill volume

  • no fracking or mining for ffs
