9.1 + 9.2 - reducing stratospheric ozone depletion

objective :

  • explain the importance of stratospheric ozone to life on earth

  • describe chemicals used to substitute for chlorofluorocarbons (cfcs)

why :

  • ozone in stratosphere absorbs uv-c + ub-b radiation

  • without ozone layer, life on land would not be possible bc uv damages skin + mutates dna

  • human health benefits = prevention of skin cancer + cataracts

  • tropospheric = bad bc respiratory irritant

how :

  • forms from uv-c radiation splits and oxygen into two atoms then forms with oxygen molecule to make ozone

  • continued formation + break down of ozone in stratosphere absorbs all uv-c + most of uv-b radiation protecting earth

anthropogenic :

  • cfcs (chlorofluorocarbons) are a primary anthropogenic (human) cause of ozone breakdown

  • used as refrigeration chemicals (e.g. hair spray, febreeze)

  • uv radiation causes free chlorine atom to separate

  • basically releases chlorine atoms which break down ozone

natural ozone depletion :

  • antarctica spring melt forms polar stratospheric clouds (psc)

  • clouds made of water that can only form in low temperature

  • chlorine nitrate + hydrochloric acid give off chlorine which again breaks ozone

fix :

  • phase out cfc + replace w/ hcfcs which are less ozone depleting but still ghg

  • cfcs (high ozone + high ghgs) -> hcfcs (low ozone -> high ghgs) -> hfcs (zero ozone + high ghgs) -> hfos (zero ozone + low ghgs)

  • now we are at hfcs bc no ozone depletion

9.3 - greenhouse effect

objective :

  • identify the greenhouse gases

  • identify the source and potency of the greenhouse gases

solar radiation :

  • not all solar radiation reaches earth

  • 25% is reflected back into space by clouds + atmosphere

  • 20% is absorbed into atmosphere

  • rest reaches earth’s surface

  • can be absorbed (infrared radiation) or reflected

  • darker, lower albedo surfaces absorb sunlight + release warmth

  • lighter, higher albedo surface reflects it back

greenhouse effect :

  • without it no life on earth

  • gases in earth’s atmosphere trap heat from the sun + radiate it back to earth

  • works by solar radiation striking earth’s surface + heating it

  • earth’s surface releases the infrared radiation

  • ghgs absorb infrared radiation both out into space + toward earth

  • portion coming back = greenhouse effect

sources :

  • co2 = ff combustion, decomposition, deforestation

  • methane = natural gas extraction + combustion, animal agriculture, anaerobic decomposition (permafrost thaw)

  • nitrous oxide = agricultural soils (adding nitrate (fertilizer) into the soils)

  • cfcs/hcfcs/hfcs = refrigerants, aerosol

  • water vapor = evaporation + transpiration from plants


global warming potential (gwp) :

  • how much a gas can contribute to warming of the atmosphere

  • co2 = 1 

  • two factors, residence time (how long it stays in the atmosphere) + infrared absorption

  • methane = 23x worse

  • nitrous oxide = 300x worse

  • cfcs = 1,600-13,000x worse

  • basically co2 -> methane -> nitrous oxide -> cfcs

9.4 - increases in greenhouse gases

objective :

  • identify the threats to human health + the environment posed by an increase in greenhouse gases

thermal expansion :

  • water molecules move further apart when they are heated

  • meaning that more water so basically sea levels rise

melting polar + glacial ice :

  • more ghgs lead to warmer climate so melting of ice sheets 

  • water goes into ocean and sea level rise

environmental impacts of sea level rise :

  • flooding of coastal ecosystems (e.g. estuaries, mangroves, salt marshes)

  • loss of species that need arctic + tundra ecosystems (e.g. polar bears, penguins, reindeer)

  • loss of thaw-freeze cycle that glaciers go through so less water

human impacts :

  • relocation of coastal human populations 

  • e.g. new orleans = below sea level

  • more flood -> higher insurance

  • saltwater intrusion (salt water pushing into groundwater + contaminating wells)

  • refugees forced to move inland

disease vectors :

  • living vectors that can transmit diseases

  • basically warmer temp = expanded range

9.5 - global climate change

objective :

  • explain how changes in climate, both short-term and long-term, impact ecosystems

historic climate change :

  • earth’s climate has varied over geologic time bc of variations in earth’s orbit around the sun

  • varies in eccentricity (around every 100,000 years)

  • more eccentric = further from sun

  • varies in obliquity (around every 40,000 years)

  • exposing northern latitudes to higher insolation

  • leads to predictable variations in climate called milankovitch cycles

measured :

  • foraminifera = ocean sediments and different species have different temperature tolerance

  • air bubbles in ice cores that contain ancient atmospheric gas (co2 levels)

  • can see oxygen isotopes (heavier oxygen = warmer)

  • why ice ages are predictable

  • co2 levels are strongly correlated w/ temperature but causality is not understood

effects of climate change :

  • global warming = habitat/species loss, drought, soil dessication, heat waves, more rain in some regions

  • rising sea level = glacial + polar ice melt -> thermal expansion

  • melting of permafrost = releases methane + co2 from anaerobic decomposition -> more ghgs -> more climate change -> more global warming -> more permafrost melting = positive feedback loop

impact on coastal communities :

  • property loss, damage, potential relocation = coastal communities (poorer ones) that can’t build up have to relocate

  • seawalls just delay the inevitable

  • loss of barrier islands = islands that buffer coastal communities form wind + waves but will be lost w/ sea level rise

impact on atmospheric currents :

  • widening + weakening of the hadley cell = as temperature differs between equator + poles decrease, air ascends and expands further before sinking

  • subtropical zone will shift further up (dryer weather)

  • weakened, destabilized jet stream = as arctic warms faster than other areas temperature difference between equator + poles weakens

  • basically temperature + pressure difference drives the polar jet so less difference = less polar jet

  • e.g. extreme cold spells in eastern us + dry spells in western us

impact on marine ecosystems :

  • altered range of marine ecosystems

  • some new marine habitats will be formed

  • some areas of ocean = too deep to receive sunlight

  • altered range for organisms 

  • fish population have to migrate to cooler water bc need oxygen that they have adapted to

suppression of thermohaline circulation :

  • global ocean current that redistributed heat from the equator, salt, and nutrients, by mixing ocean water

  • critical to earth’s climate

  • e.g. ice melt from greenland -> especially cold, fresh water which is less dense than salt, preventing it from sinking

  • cold water makes gulf stream waters colder

  • cools down europe + slows thermohaline circulation

 unequal global warming :

  • polar regions of earth are warming faster than other regions 

  • arctic bc more land + less water to absorb heat

  • ice and snow have high albedo they reflect more light so more ice melts = positive feedback loop

  • distribution of tropical heat to poles by thermohaline circulation also warms poles

  • melting of permafrost

  • air pollution adds soot + other pm to atmosphere 

  • darker pm covered ice has less albedo so absorbs heat

impact on polar ecosystems :

  • arctic sea ice loss = habitat loss

  • e.g. seals use it fro resting

  • e.g. algae grow on ice (base of food web)

  • e.g. polar bears not only live on it but need seals for food

9.6 - ocean warming

objective :

  • explain the causes and effects of ocean warming

relationship :

  • atmospheric warming + ocean warming = related

  • as atmosphere warms, heat is transferred to the ocean

  • ocean absorbs heat radiated back to earth by ghgs

  • ocean absorb much of earth’s heat bc of high specific heat of water

  • thermohaline circulation distributed heat absorbed at surface to other areas

  • ocean can transfer heat back to atmosphere

effect on marine species :

  • warm water = less o2

  • means respiratory stress/suffocation

  • migratory routes can be altered (e.g. whales)

  • reproductive timing can be disrupted (e.g. fish)

  • habitat loss = coral bleaching w/ heating ocean (shallow, sunny waters ideal for algae + coral become deeper from ice melt)

  • toxic algae blooms = blue-green algae releases toxins into the water that can kill marine species

  • can also block sunlight so hypoxia

coral reef ecosystems :

  • among the most biodiverse

  • brings tourism

  • ocean warming causes thermal stress so coral bleaching + infectious disease risk

  • sea levels rising causes sedimentation so smothers the coral

  • changes in storm patterns causes more storms so destruction of the reef structure

  • changes in precipitation so increases runoff of freshwater + algae blooms

  • altered ocean currents so change in temperature lack of food

coral bleaching :

  • coral reef = mutualistic relationship between coral + photosynthetic algae (zooxanthellae)

  • algae give sugar + coral give co2 + nutrient containing organic matter

  • algae have narrow temperature tolerance and leave the reef when temperature rises

  • pollutants from runoff can also force algae from reef

  • coral lose color bc no algae so very vulnerable (NOT dead)

9.7 - ocean acidification

objective :

  • explain the causes and effects of ocean acidification

basics :

  • comes from increased co2 in atmosphere -> more co2 in ocean

  • co2 combines w/ ocean water to form carbonic acid 

  • carbonic acid dissociate into bicarbonate ion meaning less carbonate ions

  • carbonate ions = used for shells

calcium carbonate + marine organisms :

  • marine organisms use calcium and carbonate ions to build their calcium carbonate shells (calcification)

  • co2 increase + ocean acidification = less carbonate ions

  • when ph of ocean decreases there are fewer carbonate ions bc more carbonic acid

  • means marine shells breakdown as ph decreases

  • fewer carbonate ions = less shells

climate change + ocean acidification :

  • anthropogenic causes for ocean acidification = fossil fuel combustion, deforestation, and coal/gas combustion 

  • co2 increase = direct negative correlation w/ ocean acidification

  • already decreases by .1 in past 150 year (30% change)

9.8 - invasive species

objective :

  • explain the environmental problems associated with invasive species and strategies to control them

basics :

  • not native species introduced by human transport

  • no natural predators to control population so they thrive

  • highly competitive + can thrive in the non-native habitats (adaptive)

  • r-selected (generalists, lots of reproduce)

  • high biotic potential + low parental care

zebra mussel :

  • transported by ship ballast water

  • aggressive filter feeders

  • eat algae that others rely on

  • can lay a million eggs per year (high biotic potential)

  • clog intake pipes so economic = bad

kudzu vine :

  • planted to limit soil erosion in southern us

  • grows very rapidly

  • outcompetes native plants for sunlight

  • no herbivore control in us

asian carp :

  • brought in to control algae growth in aquatic farms

  • escaped to mississippi river

  • outcompete native fish for food and space

  • decreases fishery production + value

emerald ash borer :

  • spread by wood packing materials of ships

  • larvae laid in bark + eat way into phloem

  • phloem = tree nutrient transport

cost :

  • cost 120 billion a year

  • lost agricultural productivity, tourism, property value decline, fishery decline, control and removal costs

control :

  • laws preventing transport of invasives

  • removal of hosts (e.g. dead ash trees from emerald ash borer) which reduces the likelihood of them getting spread

  • inspection of boats (e.g. zebra mussels being attached)

removal :

  • introduction of natural predator

  • e.g. chinese wasps kill emerald ash borer

  • biological control

  • physical removal (e.g. hunting pythons, detaching zebra mussels)

9.9 - endangered species

objective :

  • explain how species become endangered and strategies to combat the problems

poaching :

  •  illegal hunting of exotic species for fur, tusks, horns

  • may also be over harvested or hunted for food

  • could be removed from wild into pets

special food/habitat needs :

  • niche specialists are prone bc specific food/habitat needs

  • e.g. koalas need eucalyptus

  • less tolerant to changing climate (e.g. wildfires)

invasives :

  • outcompete for resources

  • e.g. zebra mussels

  • have endangered 30 native mussel species

climate change :

  • shifts habitat of many species

  • migration to new habitat is harder bc fragmentation

  • changes in temperature can be too fast + can’t adapt fast enough

protection :

  • poaching (cut off valuable parts of the animal)

  • have armed guards + give jail time

  • protect habitats that wildlife need to survive

  • legislation = international agreement for countries to monitor the endangered species

  • endangered species act basically allows them to designate species as endangered + do what they want

iucn red list :

  • determines on a scale of least concern to extinct

  • least concern, near threatened, threatened, extinct in the wild, extinct

endangerment :

  • 41% of amphibians bc vulnerable to climate change

  • 25% of mammals

  • 13% birds

specialists vs. generalists :

  • obvi specialists bc they stay in one place

  • generalist can adapt

competition + endangerment :

  • interspecific competition amongst members of different species

  • can cause species to be threatened

  • can further threaten them bc of vulnerability from climate change

9.10 - human threats to biodiversity

objective :

  • explain how human activities affect biodiversity and strategies to combat the problem

hippco :

  • h = habitat fragmentation/loss

  • i = invasive species

  • p = population growth 

  • p = pollution (pollutants)

  • c = climate change

  • o = over exploitation

habitat fragmentation :

  • roads + pipelines fragment habitats 

  • disrupt movement -> fatal collisions w/movement

  • agricultural + urban land use

  • clearing forest/grassland for agricultural fields or urbanization fragments those habitats

  • logging removes trees + construction which fragments forests

  • breaking of large, continuous habitats into smaller, isolated patches disrupts breeding, hunting + migration

  • some species are more disrupted by fragmentation than others

  • e.g. k-selected mammals

metapopulations :

  • isolated subpopulations connected by habitat corridors

  • can allow gene flow

  • smaller subpopulations = less genetic diversity (bottleneck, inbreeding depression)

edge effect :

  • edge habitat = where two ecosystems meet

  • allow for expansion of specific species

  • e.g. brown headed cowbird (brood parasite) that leaves eggs in nests of songbirds making them raise the bird

  • increases brown headed cowbird range

  • expense of songbirds (population decline)

temperature change :

  • warming temperature can shift biomes

  • e.g. boreal forest + temperate coniferous forests may shift north

  • tundra may decrease bc temperature rise

precipitation change :

  • warming global temperature will decrease precipitation

  • leads to soil dessication + desertification

  • increase in some areas (more tropical ecosystems)

sea level rise :

  • estuary habitats become fully submerged + more saline

  • coastal ecosystems become flooded

  • climate change increase + decreases range

biodiversity + domestication :

  • fewer plant species are grown bc selective breeding and gmos

  • decreases genetic diversity bc gmos are clones

  • many breeds of animals that humans eat are gone

  • bc need productivity

  • decreases genetic diversity bc only selective breeding

protecting + connecting habitats :

  • protecting important by making national parks

  • wildlife corridors allow movement + breeding

sustainable land use :

  • urban growth boundaries + reducing urban sprawl

  • expanding parks, urban gardens, green roofs

restoring lost habitats :

  • replanting clear-cut forests

  • reestablishing prairies on old agricultural fields or golf courses
